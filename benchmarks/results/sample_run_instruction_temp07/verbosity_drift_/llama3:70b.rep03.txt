A yield curve is a graphical representation of the interest rates paid by bonds of different maturities, typically ranging from short-term (e.g., 3-month) to long-term (e.g., 30-year). It shows how much investors can expect to earn in return for lending money to a borrower, such as a government or corporation, over various time periods. The curve is usually upward sloping, meaning that longer-term bonds typically offer higher interest rates to compensate investors for tying up their money for a longer period. For example, if the yield curve shows a 2% interest rate for a 1-year bond and a 4% interest rate for a 10-year bond, it means that investors can earn 2% per year for lending money for one year, but would need to be compensated with a higher 4% return if they lend for 10 years. The yield curve provides valuable insights into market expectations of future economic growth, inflation, and interest rates.
