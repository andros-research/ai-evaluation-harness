A yield curve is a graphical representation of the interest rates at which investors are willing to lend money to borrowers with different time horizons. It's essentially a snapshot of the market's expectations about future borrowing costs and returns on investments. The horizontal axis shows the time period, ranging from short-term (e.g., overnight) to long-term (e.g., 30 years), while the vertical axis displays the interest rate or yield that borrowers are paying for those loan periods. A normal yield curve slopes upward, meaning that longer-term debts typically have higher yields than shorter-term ones, reflecting investors' willingness to accept more risk and less liquidity in exchange for potentially higher returns over a longer period. This information is crucial for financial markets, as it helps guide investment decisions, inform borrowing rates, and even influence economic growth.
