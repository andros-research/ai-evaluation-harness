 A yield curve is a graph that shows the interest rates, or yields, of bonds with different maturities (time to maturity) offered by the same issuer at a specific point in time. Longer-term bonds generally have higher yields because they come with a greater risk for the lender: the longer the investment period, the more uncertain events like inflation, interest rate changes, or default could impact the return on investment. On a yield curve, these long-term bonds would be located to the right (higher) of shorter-term bonds, creating an upward slope. However, in some cases, the yield curve can also be downward sloping or "inverted," meaning short-term bonds have higher yields than long-term ones. An inverted yield curve is often seen as a predictor of economic slowdown or recession because it suggests that investors expect short-term interest rates to rise faster than long-term ones, potentially leading to reduced borrowing and investing by businesses and consumers.
