A yield curve is a graphical representation of the relationship between the interest rates (or yields) offered by bonds of different maturity periods and their corresponding prices. Essentially, it's a chart that shows how much return investors can expect to earn from lending money to a borrower (like a government or company) for varying lengths of time. The curve plots the interest rate on the vertical axis against the time to maturity on the horizontal axis, typically ranging from short-term debt (e.g., 3-month treasury bills) to long-term debt (e.g., 30-year bonds). A normal yield curve slopes upward, meaning that investors demand higher returns for tying up their money for longer periods. This makes sense, as they're taking on more risk and giving up liquidity. The shape and slope of the yield curve can provide valuable insights into market expectations about future economic growth, inflation, and interest rates.
