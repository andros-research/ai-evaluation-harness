A yield curve is a graphical representation of the relationship between interest rates and time to maturity on bonds with similar credit quality. It's essentially a snapshot of the market's expectations about future interest rates. The vertical axis shows the yield or interest rate, while the horizontal axis represents the bond's maturity date. A normal yield curve slopes upward, meaning shorter-term bonds (like 1-year notes) have lower yields than longer-term bonds (like 10-year notes), as investors demand a higher return to lock in their money for a longer period. This makes sense, as you'd expect to earn more interest over time to compensate for inflation and the risk of default. A yield curve can also be inverted, where shorter-term rates are higher than longer-term rates, indicating market uncertainty or a potential recession.
