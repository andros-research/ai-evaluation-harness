A yield curve is a graphical representation of the interest rates on bonds with different maturities (lengths of time until they're paid off). It's like a snapshot of the market's expectations about how much return investors can earn from lending money at various times in the future. On the x-axis, you have the maturity dates (e.g., 1 year, 2 years, 5 years), and on the y-axis, you have the corresponding interest rates (yields) that investors would demand for each bond. A normal yield curve slopes upward, meaning shorter-term bonds typically offer lower yields than longer-term ones. This reflects the general idea that investors want to be compensated more for taking on more risk or waiting longer to get their money back.
