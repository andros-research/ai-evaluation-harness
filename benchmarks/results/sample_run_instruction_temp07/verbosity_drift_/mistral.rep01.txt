 A yield curve is a graphical representation of the interest rates associated with various maturities or durations of bonds issued by a single entity (like a government or corporation). It plots the interest rate, or yield, on a bond versus its term to maturity. Normally, longer-term bonds have higher yields because investors demand more compensation for tying up their money for a longer period and facing greater uncertainty about the issuer's ability to repay. A "normal" yield curve is upward sloping, reflecting this relationship. However, an inverted yield curve (where short-term rates are higher than long-term rates) can be a sign of economic trouble as it suggests that investors expect short-term interest rates to rise above long-term rates in the future, potentially indicating a slowdown or recession.
