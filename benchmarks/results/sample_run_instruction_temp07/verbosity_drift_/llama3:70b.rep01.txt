A yield curve is a graphical representation of the relationship between the interest rates (or yields) offered by bonds of different maturities. Essentially, it shows how much investors can expect to earn from lending money to a borrower (like a government or company) for varying periods of time. The curve plots the yield on the vertical axis against the time to maturity on the horizontal axis, typically ranging from short-term debt (e.g., 3-month bills) to long-term debt (e.g., 30-year bonds). A normal yield curve slopes upward, indicating that investors demand higher returns for tying up their money for longer periods. This makes sense, as they're taking on more risk and giving up the opportunity to earn interest elsewhere. The shape and slope of the yield curve can provide valuable insights into market expectations about inflation, economic growth, and interest rates, making it a closely watched indicator in finance.
